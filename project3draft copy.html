Africa's Electricity Crisis

Six Uncomfortable Truths

If income and electrification have increased across Africa, why do Africans still consume so little electricity?

Project aim: This project dismantles comforting narratives about progress. High access rates mask profound consumption inequality. Grid connections don't guarantee usable electricity when systems are unaffordable, unreliable, or constrained by policy failures. Each visualization answers a necessary sub-question, building from symptoms to mechanisms to structural explanations.

Six visualizations reveal the truth: Africa's electricity crisis is not a lack of connections, income, or resources. It is a failure of systems—how electricity is priced, governed, and delivered.

01

The Divide

Does electricity access reflect real electricity use?

While access rates suggest substantial progress across Africa, electricity consumption remains extremely low and uneven. Many countries with widespread grid connections still consume too little power to support basic modern living standards.

02

Living on 3 Lightbulbs

How much electricity do Africans actually use?

This horizontal bar chart ranks Sub-Saharan African countries by electricity consumption per capita. The reference line marks the World Bank's 1,000 kWh threshold for basic modern energy use—enough to power refrigeration, productive appliances, and continuous lighting. Most SSA countries fall far below this threshold.

03

The Injustice

Poorest Countries Pay 4× More for Electricity

Question: Is low electricity use simply a result of poverty?

This scatter plot reveals the affordability paradox: GDP per capita plotted against residential electricity tariffs shows that poorer populations often pay higher rates. Bubble size represents population, color indicates sub-region. Use the dropdown to filter by region and explore the inverse relationship creating a poverty trap.

Key Insights

Liberia charges $0.40/kWh for populations earning $1.70/day (24% of daily income per kWh). Sierra Leone charges $0.35/kWh at $2.10/day (17%). South Africa charges $0.12/kWh at $16/day (0.75%). A Liberian works 4 hours to afford what a South African buys in 5 minutes. The poverty trap: low consumption → high fixed costs → unaffordable tariffs → lower consumption. Weak utilities can't achieve scale economies.

Data Source: World Bank WDI (GDP per capita PPP), Global Petrol Prices (residential electricity tariffs $/kWh), IEA / National Regulators, 2023-2024

04

The Hidden Economy

When the Grid Fails, Africa Generates Its Own Power

Question: How do households and firms cope when the grid doesn't deliver?

This grouped bar chart compares official grid electricity generation against estimated diesel self-generation by businesses. Grid unreliability forces firms to create an invisible parallel energy system using expensive, polluting diesel generators. This shadow economy doesn't appear in official statistics but drives up production costs and carbon emissions.

Key Insights

Nigerian businesses generate approximately 43% as much electricity as the national grid via diesel generators. In Liberia and Sierra Leone, 70-80% of firms own backup generators. Hidden costs: diesel generation costs $0.30-0.50/kWh versus grid $0.10-0.15/kWh, raising production costs and pricing African manufacturers out of global markets. Environmental impact: diesel emits 2-3× more CO₂ per kWh than grid electricity, canceling renewable gains.

Data Source: IEA Statistics / World Bank (official grid generation GWh), World Bank Enterprise Surveys (% firms owning/sharing backup generators)

05

The Paradox

Africa Has the Resources, Not the Infrastructure

Question: Does Africa lack energy resources—or the systems to use them?

This side-by-side visualization reveals the brutal disconnect between potential and reality. On the left: what Africa has actually deployed in renewable capacity—pathetically small bubbles. On the right: the abundant renewable energy potential nature provided—rich coverage across the continent. The gap between reality and possibility is staggering.

Installed Renewable Capacity

Bubble size = total renewable capacity (MW). Color = dominant renewable source.

Renewable Energy Potential

Combined solar, wind, and hydro potential (TWh/year). Darker colors = greater resource endowment.

Key Insights

Sub-Saharan Africa possesses world-class renewable resources: 5-6 kWh/m²/day solar irradiance (world's highest), strong wind corridors, and 1,000+ GW untapped hydro potential. Yet total renewable deployment across SSA is less than 50 GW for 1.2 billion people (40W/capita) versus Germany's 140 GW for 84 million (1,670W/capita). Nigeria has solar potential matching Saudi Arabia but less than 0.2 GW deployed. The crisis isn't resource scarcity—it's investment barriers, policy uncertainty, and institutional failure.

Data Source: IRENA Renewable Capacity Statistics 2024, Global Solar Atlas, Global Wind Atlas, World Bank (population)

06

The Divergence

Some Escaping, Others Trapped

Question: Do electricity outcomes improve automatically as countries grow richer?

This animated scatter plot tracks countries over 13 years (2010-2023), plotting GDP per capita against electricity consumption per capita. Use the year slider to watch trajectories unfold. Some countries escape energy poverty while others remain trapped despite GDP growth. Economic growth does not guarantee rising electricity use—outcomes diverge persistently over time.

Key Insights

Kenya doubled consumption 150→350 kWh through integrated grid and off-grid programs. Rwanda tripled 50→145 kWh despite starting from genocide. Ethiopia grew through massive hydro investment. Meanwhile, Nigeria stagnated 145→160 kWh despite 4× Kenya's GDP per capita and oil revenues. Angola and DRC show similar patterns: GDP growth without electricity investment equals hollow development. The gap between Escapers and Trapped widened 2010-2023, not closed. Policy determines trajectories.

Data Source: World Bank World Development Indicators - GDP per capita PPP, Electric power consumption per capita, Population, Annual data 2010-2023

Conclusions

The Crisis is Not Inevitable

Africa's electricity challenge is not about lacking connections, income, or resources. It is about how electricity systems are priced, governed, and delivered. Six uncomfortable truths reveal the gap between perception and reality:

  1. Access looks high → but hides deprivation (Chart 1: The Divide)
  2. Consumption is extremely low → modern energy is absent (Chart 2: Living on 3 Lightbulbs)
  3. Poverty alone cannot explain this → tariffs matter (Chart 3: The Injustice)
  4. System failure suppresses demand → reliability matters (Chart 4: The Hidden Economy)
  5. Resources are abundant → the constraint lies elsewhere (Chart 5: The Paradox)
  6. Time does not fix the problem → divergence persists (Chart 6: The Divergence)

Three Critical Findings

1

Access Percentages Mislead

Consumption inequality dwarfs connection inequality. South Africa's 4,000 kWh/capita versus Chad's 132 kWh/capita represents a 30× gap invisible in access rates. Policy should target minimum consumption thresholds (500 kWh/capita for basic productive use) not just connection counts.

2

Affordability and Reliability Crises Render Connections Unusable

Liberia charging $0.40/kWh for populations earning less than $4/day. Nigeria with 78% of firms self-generating through expensive diesel. Tariff reform and reliability investment matter more than expanding unreliable, unaffordable systems.

3

Investment Follows Institutional Capacity, Not Resource Potential

Nigeria's renewable underinvestment despite 5.5 kWh/m²/day solar irradiance reflects policy failure, not technical constraint. Chart 6's temporal dimension shows gaps widened 2010-2023, not converged—some countries escaped (Kenya, Rwanda, Ethiopia) through policy reform, others stagnated (Nigeria, DRC, Angola) despite GDP growth and resource wealth.

Together, these charts demonstrate that Africa's electricity crisis is not inevitable. Understanding that the problem is structural—about systems, not scarcity—is the first step toward designing solutions that actually work.

Data & Tools

Data Sources

This project relies entirely on publicly accessible, open data from international organizations and research institutions. Primary sources include: World Bank World Development Indicators (electricity access, consumption, GDP, population 2010-2023), International Energy Agency Statistics (tariffs, generation mix), IRENA Renewable Capacity Statistics 2024 (solar, wind, hydro installed capacity), World Bank Enterprise Surveys (generator ownership rates 2015-2023), Global Solar Atlas and Global Wind Atlas (renewable resource potential).

Tools & Technologies

All visualizations built in Vega-Lite 5.x, embedded in HTML via vegaEmbed. Data processing workflow implemented in Python (pandas for data manipulation, statsmodels for regression analysis). Complete data processing pipeline documented in public Google Colab notebook. All chart specifications and processed datasets hosted on GitHub repository for full reproducibility.

Analytical Approach

Key methodological decisions: (1) Consumption per capita preferred over total generation because it accounts for population size and reveals inequality invisible in aggregate figures. (2) Generator ownership as reliability proxy—Enterprise Survey data on backup generators provides standardized measure of grid failure across countries. (3) Logarithmic scaling for GDP addresses right-skewed income distribution. (4) Regression analysis uses OLS with robust standard errors. (5) Temporal classification based on electricity-GDP elasticity thresholds from development literature.
