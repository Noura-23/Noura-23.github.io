Africa's Electricity Crisis

Six Uncomfortable Truths

If electricity access and household incomes have both risen across Africa, why does per capita electricity consumption remain so low?

Project aim: This analysis challenges three prevailing narratives: that rising access rates signal meaningful electrification, that economic growth automatically expands energy consumption, and that Africa's electricity deficit stems from resource scarcity. Six visualizations move systematically from symptoms to mechanisms to structural explanations, demonstrating that the crisis reflects failures in how electricity is priced, governed, and delivered

01

The Divide

Does electricity access reflect real electricity use?

While access rates suggest substantial progress across Africa, electricity consumption remains extremely low and uneven. Many countries with widespread grid connections still consume very little electricity.

Use the dropdown to reveal the gap between electricity access (% of population) and electricity consumption (kWh per capita).

But what does this gap look like once we measure electricity use directly?

02

Living on 3 Lightbulbs

How much electricity do Africans actually use?

Most African countries fall far below the Modern Energy Minimum of 1,000 kWh per person per year. Consumption levels are heavily concentrated at the lower end of the distribution, with only a few countries approaching the benchmark.

Hover over the bars to see electricity consumption (kWh per capita) for each country.

If consumption is this low even where access exists, the explanation cannot be access alone. Why, then, do people consume so little electricity?

03

The Injustice

Is low electricity use simply a result of poverty?

Rather than falling with income, electricity prices are often highest in the poorest countries. High tariffs make electricity unaffordable for households and firms.

Hover over the bubbles to see the country, GDP per capita, and electricity tariff.

But high prices are only part of the story. What happens when electricity is not only expensive but unreliable?

04

The Hidden Economy

How do households and firms cope when the grid doesn't deliver?

In many African countries, unreliable grid electricity leads firms to rely on backup generators. This chart shows a strong relationship between grid reliability and generator ownership: where outages are more frequent, firms are far more likely to self-generate power. This parallel system reduces reliance on grid electricity.

Hover over the points to see actual and predicted generator ownership, and the share of firms experiencing outages by country.

High prices limit electricity consumption, and unreliable grids push firms off the grid. Is the remaining constraint a lack of energy resources or a failure to deploy them?

05

The Paradox

Does Africa lack energy resources or the systems to use them?

Solar abundance does not translate into deployment. This correlation analysis shows that solar resource quality is only weakly associated with installed capacity, while deployment aligns more closely with income levels suggesting that electricity scarcity reflects system and investment constraints rather than natural resource limits.

Hover over the cells to view correlations.

Given that deployment aligns more closely with income than solar resources, the next question becomes: Do electricity outcomes improve automatically as countries grow richer?

06

The Divergence

Do electricity outcomes improve automatically as countries grow richer?

Some countries steadily expand electricity consumption as they grow richer, while others stagnate despite rising incomes. Rather than converging toward similar outcomes, these paths diverge showing that economic growth alone does not guarantee electricity expansion.

Hover over the points to view GDP per capita and electric power consumption by country, and use the year slider to explore patterns from 2000 to 2023.

Together, these patterns reveal that Africa's electricity crisis is structural, not transitional. If neither resource endowment nor income growth alone determines outcomes, the divergence must stem from differences in how countries govern, invest in, and deploy their energy systems.

Data & Tools

Data Sources

This project relies entirely on publicly accessible, open data from international organizations and research institutions. Primary sources include: World Bank World Development Indicators (electricity access, consumption, GDP, population 2010-2023), International Energy Agency Statistics (tariffs, generation mix), IRENA Renewable Capacity Statistics 2024 (solar, wind, hydro installed capacity), World Bank Enterprise Surveys (generator ownership rates 2015-2023), Global Solar Atlas and Global Wind Atlas (renewable resource potential).

Tools & Technologies

All visualizations built in Vega-Lite 5.x, embedded in HTML via vegaEmbed. Data processing workflow implemented in Python (pandas for data manipulation, statsmodels for regression analysis). Complete data processing pipeline documented in public Google Colab notebook. All chart specifications and processed datasets hosted on GitHub repository for full reproducibility.

Analytical Approach

Key methodological decisions: (1) Consumption per capita preferred over total generation because it accounts for population size and reveals inequality invisible in aggregate figures. (5) Temporal classification based on electricity-GDP elasticity thresholds from development literature.
