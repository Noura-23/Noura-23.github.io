PP434 - Automated Visualisation for Policymaking

Africa's Electricity Crisis

Six Uncomfortable Truths

If income and electrification have increased across Africa, why do Africans still consume so little electricity?

Project aim: This project dismantles comforting narratives about progress. High access rates mask profound consumption inequality. Grid connections don't guarantee usable electricity when systems are unaffordable, unreliable, or constrained by policy failures. Each visualization answers a necessary sub-question, building from symptoms to mechanisms to structural explanations.

Six visualizations reveal the truth: Africa's electricity crisis is not a lack of connections, income, or resources. It is a failure of systems—how electricity is priced, governed, and delivered.

01

The Divide

Does electricity access reflect real electricity use?

While access rates suggest substantial progress across Africa, electricity consumption remains extremely low and uneven. Many countries with widespread grid connections still consume too little power to support basic modern living standards.

This gap between connection and use raises a deeper question: how much electricity do Africans actually consume?

02

Living on 3 Lightbulbs

How much electricity do Africans actually use?

Most countries fall far below the World Bank's 1,000 kWh threshold for basic modern energy use, indicating that electricity use is largely limited to lighting and minimal household needs rather than refrigeration, heating, or productive appliances.

If consumption is this low even where access exists, the explanation cannot be access alone. Why, then, do people consume so little electricity?

03

The Injustice

Is low electricity use simply a result of poverty?

Rather than falling with income, electricity prices are often highest in the poorest countries. High tariffs make electricity unaffordable for households and firms, sharply constraining demand even when grid connections exist.

But high prices are only part of the story. What happens when electricity is not only expensive—but unreliable?

04

The Hidden Economy

How do households and firms cope when the grid doesn't deliver?

In many countries, firms produce a large share of their own electricity using costly backup generators. This invisible parallel system raises production costs, increases emissions, and weakens trust in the formal grid—further suppressing demand for grid electricity.

If people and firms are exiting the grid, the problem may not be demand at all. Does Africa simply lack the energy resources to power its economies?

05

The Paradox

Does Africa lack energy resources—or the systems to use them?

Despite some of the world's highest solar, hydro, and wind resources, deployed capacity remains extremely limited. The disconnect between potential and deployment suggests that Africa's electricity crisis is not driven by natural scarcity, but by failures in infrastructure, investment, and systems.

If resources are abundant, and demand exists, why do some countries manage to escape electricity scarcity while others remain trapped?

06

The Divergence

Do electricity outcomes improve automatically as countries grow richer?

Some countries steadily expand electricity use as incomes rise, while others stagnate despite economic growth. Rather than converging, electricity outcomes diverge—indicating that time and growth alone do not resolve the problem.

Together, these trajectories show that Africa’s electricity crisis is structural, not transitional—and shaped by policy choices rather than inevitability.

Conclusions

The Crisis is Not Inevitable

Africa's electricity challenge is not about lacking connections, income, or resources. It is about how electricity systems are priced, governed, and delivered. Six uncomfortable truths reveal the gap between perception and reality:

  1. Access looks high → but hides deprivation (Chart 1: The Divide)
  2. Consumption is extremely low → modern energy is absent (Chart 2: Living on 3 Lightbulbs)
  3. Poverty alone cannot explain this → tariffs matter (Chart 3: The Injustice)
  4. System failure suppresses demand → reliability matters (Chart 4: The Hidden Economy)
  5. Resources are abundant → the constraint lies elsewhere (Chart 5: The Paradox)
  6. Time does not fix the problem → divergence persists (Chart 6: The Divergence)

Three Critical Findings

1

Access Percentages Mislead

Consumption inequality dwarfs connection inequality. South Africa's 4,000 kWh/capita versus Chad's 132 kWh/capita represents a 30× gap invisible in access rates. Policy should target minimum consumption thresholds (500 kWh/capita for basic productive use) not just connection counts.

2

Affordability and Reliability Crises Render Connections Unusable

Liberia charging $0.40/kWh for populations earning less than $4/day. Nigeria with 78% of firms self-generating through expensive diesel. Tariff reform and reliability investment matter more than expanding unreliable, unaffordable systems.

3

Investment Follows Institutional Capacity, Not Resource Potential

Nigeria's renewable underinvestment despite 5.5 kWh/m²/day solar irradiance reflects policy failure, not technical constraint. Chart 6's temporal dimension shows gaps widened 2010-2023, not converged—some countries escaped (Kenya, Rwanda, Ethiopia) through policy reform, others stagnated (Nigeria, DRC, Angola) despite GDP growth and resource wealth.

Together, these charts demonstrate that Africa's electricity crisis is not inevitable. Understanding that the problem is structural—about systems, not scarcity—is the first step toward designing solutions that actually work.

Data & Tools

Data Sources

This project relies entirely on publicly accessible, open data from international organizations and research institutions. Primary sources include: World Bank World Development Indicators (electricity access, consumption, GDP, population 2010-2023), International Energy Agency Statistics (tariffs, generation mix), IRENA Renewable Capacity Statistics 2024 (solar, wind, hydro installed capacity), World Bank Enterprise Surveys (generator ownership rates 2015-2023), Global Solar Atlas and Global Wind Atlas (renewable resource potential).

Tools & Technologies

All visualizations built in Vega-Lite 5.x, embedded in HTML via vegaEmbed. Data processing workflow implemented in Python (pandas for data manipulation, statsmodels for regression analysis). Complete data processing pipeline documented in public Google Colab notebook. All chart specifications and processed datasets hosted on GitHub repository for full reproducibility.

Analytical Approach

Key methodological decisions: (1) Consumption per capita preferred over total generation because it accounts for population size and reveals inequality invisible in aggregate figures. (2) Generator ownership as reliability proxy—Enterprise Survey data on backup generators provides standardized measure of grid failure across countries. (3) Logarithmic scaling for GDP addresses right-skewed income distribution. (4) Regression analysis uses OLS with robust standard errors. (5) Temporal classification based on electricity-GDP elasticity thresholds from development literature.
