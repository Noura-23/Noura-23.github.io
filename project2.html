The Paradox

Africa Has the Sun, Not the Solar

[PLACEHOLDER: 2-3 sentences explaining the resource vs. deployment disconnect. Africa has world-class solar resources but minimal deployment. This reveals policy/investment failure, not resource scarcity.]

Key Insights

[PLACEHOLDER: SSA receives 5-6 kWh/m²/day solar irradiance—world's highest. Yet <15 GW total solar for 1.2B people (12W/capita) vs Germany 70 GW for 84M (830W/capita) despite 40% lower irradiance. Nigeria: 220M people, 5.5 kWh/m²/day (matching Saudi Arabia), <200 MW solar (0.9W/capita). Kenya and South Africa prove deployment possible but remain exceptions. Crisis isn't resource scarcity—Africa is sun-rich but solar-poor due to investment barriers and policy uncertainty.]

Conclusions

Policy, Not Geography, Determines Outcomes

[PLACEHOLDER: ~250 words on key findings and policy implications]

Three Key Findings:

1

[PLACEHOLDER: Access percentages mislead—consumption inequality dwarfs connection inequality. South Africa 4,000 vs Chad 132 kWh/capita = 30× gap invisible in access rates. Policy should target minimum consumption thresholds (500 kWh/capita for basic productive use) not just connection counts.]

2

[PLACEHOLDER: Affordability and reliability crises render connections unusable. Liberia $0.40/kWh for <$4/day populations, Nigeria 78% firms self-generating. Tariff reform (cross-subsidies, lifeline rates) and reliability investment (grid maintenance) matter more than expanding unreliable, unaffordable systems.]

3

[PLACEHOLDER: Investment follows institutional capacity not resource potential. Nigeria's renewable underinvestment despite 5.5 kWh/m²/day reflects policy failure not technical constraint. Chart 6 temporal dimension shows gaps widened 2010-2023 not converged—some escaped (Kenya, Rwanda) through grid + off-grid strategies, others stagnated (Nigeria, DRC) despite GDP growth. Electricity-GDP decoupling confirms energy as binding growth constraint.]

Policy Implications:

[PLACEHOLDER: International development frameworks (SDG7, AfDB New Deal on Energy) should incorporate consumption, affordability, and reliability metrics alongside access targets. Otherwise we celebrate 100% connections while populations remain energy-poor. Need differentiated strategies: grid extension where viable, off-grid solutions where cost-effective, but always with focus on sufficiency not just access.]
