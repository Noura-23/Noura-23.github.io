Africa's Electricity Crisis

Six Uncomfortable Truths

Despite rising incomes, expanding grid connections, and abundant natural resources, most Africans still consume extremely little electricity. This project investigates why electricity deprivation persists across Sub-Saharan Africa, moving beyond access statistics to examine consumption inequality, affordability barriers, grid reliability failures, and long-term divergence patterns. It's deliberately diagnostic rather than prescriptive—challenging intuitions and identifying where the problem truly lies.

↓ Scroll to explore

Aims

Core Research Question

If income and electrification have increased across Africa, why do Africans still consume so little electricity?

This project dismantles comforting narratives about progress. High access rates mask profound consumption inequality. Grid connections don't guarantee usable electricity when systems are unaffordable, unreliable, or constrained by policy failures. Each visualization answers a necessary sub-question, building from symptoms to mechanisms to structural explanations.

Six visualizations reveal the truth: Africa's electricity crisis is not a lack of connections, income, or resources. It is a failure of systems—how electricity is priced, governed, and delivered.

01

The Divide

Where Africa's Lights Stay On

Question: Does electricity access reflect real electricity use?

This interactive map allows you to switch between two views of the same geography: electricity access (% of population connected) and electricity consumption (kWh per capita). While access rates suggest widespread progress, consumption levels reveal stark inequality and deprivation across the continent. Toggle between views to see the transformation.

Key Insights

Being connected to the grid does not mean having usable electricity. Nigeria shows 60% access but only 156 kWh/capita consumption, while Chad shows 12% access yet 132 kWh/capita—the disconnect is profound. Southern Africa clusters at 3,000-4,000 kWh/capita while West Africa remains below 200 kWh despite similar access rates.

Role in narrative: This chart dismantles the comforting narrative that Africa's electricity problem is close to being solved. Access percentages mislead—they hide consumption inequality.

02

Living on 3 Lightbulbs

What Africa's Electricity Actually Powers

Question: How much electricity do Africans actually use?

This horizontal bar chart ranks Sub-Saharan African countries by electricity consumption per capita. The reference line marks the World Bank's 1,000 kWh threshold for basic modern energy use—enough to power refrigeration, productive appliances, and continuous lighting. Most SSA countries fall far below this threshold.

Key Insights

23 of 25 SSA countries consume less than 1,000 kWh/capita—below what's needed for basic modern life. Chad's 132 kWh equals approximately 3 hours of lighting per day. Nigeria's 156 kWh for 220 million people cannot power a refrigerator continuously. South Africa at 3,900 kWh consumes 30× more than Chad. Most "connected" Africans live in pre-refrigeration conditions.

Role in narrative: This chart establishes the depth of the electricity deficit, moving from illusion (access) to reality (consumption). Electricity in much of Africa supports lighting, not modern life.

03

The Injustice

Poorest Countries Pay 4× More for Electricity

Question: Is low electricity use simply a result of poverty?

This scatter plot reveals the affordability paradox: GDP per capita plotted against residential electricity tariffs shows that poorer populations often pay higher rates. Bubble size represents population, color indicates sub-region. Use the dropdown to filter by region and explore the inverse relationship creating a poverty trap.

Key Insights

Liberia charges $0.40/kWh for populations earning $1.70/day (24% of daily income per kWh). Sierra Leone charges $0.35/kWh at $2.10/day (17%). South Africa charges $0.12/kWh at $16/day (0.75%). A Liberian works 4 hours to afford what a South African buys in 5 minutes. The poverty trap: low consumption → high fixed costs → unaffordable tariffs → lower consumption. Weak utilities can't achieve scale economies.

Role in narrative: This chart challenges the idea that income alone explains low electricity use and introduces pricing and affordability as key constraints separate from poverty. Electricity poverty often begins at the tariff.

04

The Hidden Economy

When the Grid Fails, Africa Generates Its Own Power

Question: How do households and firms cope when the grid doesn't deliver?

This grouped bar chart compares official grid electricity generation against estimated diesel self-generation by businesses. Grid unreliability forces firms to create an invisible parallel energy system using expensive, polluting diesel generators. This shadow economy doesn't appear in official statistics but drives up production costs and carbon emissions.

Key Insights

Nigerian businesses generate approximately 43% as much electricity as the national grid via diesel generators. In Liberia and Sierra Leone, 70-80% of firms own backup generators. Hidden costs: diesel generation costs $0.30-0.50/kWh versus grid $0.10-0.15/kWh, raising production costs and pricing African manufacturers out of global markets. Environmental impact: diesel emits 2-3× more CO₂ per kWh than grid electricity, canceling renewable gains.

Role in narrative: When the grid fails, people exit the system. This chart explains the mechanism behind persistent low consumption—unreliable systems suppress demand and erode trust in formal electricity infrastructure.

Methodology & Data Challenges

Methodology

[PLACEHOLDER: Diesel generation estimated using multi-step calculation: (1) % firms owning/sharing generators from World Bank Enterprise Surveys, (2) assumed average generator capacity of 50 kW per firm (conservative based on typical commercial backup units), (3) estimated utilization of 6 hours/day based on reported average outage duration in surveys, (4) scaled to total registered firms using World Bank Doing Business data, (5) annualized to GWh. Formula: Diesel_GWh = (Firms × Generator_Ownership% × 50kW × 6hrs × 365days) / 1,000,000. Official grid generation from IEA/World Bank electricity statistics. Grouped bars allow direct visual comparison of grid vs diesel for each country.]

Data Challenges

[PLACEHOLDER: This is the most methodologically challenging chart in the project. Enterprise Survey data covers formal registered firms only—misses informal sector and household generators (likely substantial). Survey samples typically 300-1,500 firms per country, not nationally representative. Survey years vary (2015-2023), creating temporal misalignment with 2022-2023 grid data. Generator capacity assumption (50 kW) is rough average—actual range 10-500 kW depending on firm size. Utilization hours (6/day) based on outage duration but actual runtime may differ. "Firms" count from Doing Business database includes wide definition (all registered businesses). Cross-validation attempted using diesel import statistics but challenging to separate generator fuel from transport/other uses. Estimates are deliberately conservative—likely understate true parallel generation. Some countries lack recent Enterprise Survey data (e.g., DRC 2013, Angola 2010), requiring use of dated generator ownership rates. Result should be interpreted as order-of-magnitude indicator, not precise measurement.]

Data Sources: IEA Statistics / World Bank (official grid generation GWh), World Bank Enterprise Surveys (% firms owning/sharing backup generators), World Bank Doing Business (registered firms count), diesel estimates calculated from firm ownership rates

Access: IEA Statistics | World Bank Enterprise Surveys

05

The Paradox

Africa Has the Resources, Not the Infrastructure

Question: Does Africa lack energy resources—or the systems to use them?

This side-by-side visualization reveals the brutal disconnect between potential and reality. On the left: what Africa has actually deployed in renewable capacity—pathetically small bubbles. On the right: the abundant renewable energy potential nature provided—rich purple coverage across the continent. The gap between reality and possibility is staggering.

Installed Renewable Capacity

Bubble size = total renewable capacity (MW). Color = dominant renewable source (blue=hydro, yellow=solar, green=wind, purple=mixed). Most SSA countries have tiny bubbles clustered at low per capita values.

Renewable Energy Potential

Combined solar, wind, and hydro potential (TWh/year). Darker purple = greater natural resource endowment. Most of SSA shows dark purple—abundant potential.

Key Insights

Sub-Saharan Africa possesses world-class renewable resources: 5-6 kWh/m²/day solar irradiance (world's highest), strong wind corridors in East and Southern Africa, and 1,000+ GW untapped hydro potential. Yet total renewable deployment across SSA is less than 50 GW for 1.2 billion people (40W/capita) versus Germany's 140 GW for 84 million (1,670W/capita). Nigeria has solar potential matching Saudi Arabia but less than 0.2 GW deployed. DRC has Africa's largest hydro potential (100 GW) but only 2.5 GW installed. The crisis isn't resource scarcity—it's investment barriers, policy uncertainty, and institutional failure.

Role in narrative: This final chart reframes the problem from scarcity to systems failure. The electricity crisis is not a resource constraint—the constraint lies in coordination, institutions, and policy design. Opens the door to future research.

Methodology & Data Challenges

Methodology

[PLACEHOLDER: LEFT CHART - Bubble chart plots countries by per capita capacity (W/person) on logarithmic x-axis to show massive range. Bubble size encodes total capacity (MW) using square root scale. Color indicates dominant source: if >50% of capacity is hydro=blue, solar=yellow, wind=green, no dominant source=purple (mixed). Includes 2-3 reference countries (Germany, Saudi Arabia) for comparison. RIGHT CHART - Choropleth uses log scale color gradient (light to dark purple) for combined renewable potential in TWh/year. Potential calculated as: Solar (kWh/m²/day × land area × efficiency factor 0.15 × 365), Wind (technical potential from Global Wind Atlas), Hydro (economically feasible potential from IRENA/World Bank assessments). Countries matched to TopoJSON using ISO codes.]

Data Challenges

[PLACEHOLDER: CAPACITY DATA - IRENA reports installed capacity by technology but some small installations (<1 MW) may be unreported. Off-grid solar systems (home systems, mini-grids) often not included in official capacity statistics—this significantly understates Kenya, Tanzania, Rwanda solar deployment. Capacity factor differences not shown: 1 MW hydro ≠ 1 MW solar in actual generation. POTENTIAL DATA - Solar potential estimates vary by source (Global Solar Atlas vs NREL) based on different efficiency assumptions and exclusion criteria (protected areas, slopes, urban areas). We used conservative estimates. Wind potential highly variable by location—national averages mask regional concentrations. Hydro potential includes large projects that may be environmentally/socially controversial (e.g., Grand Inga in DRC). "Potential" doesn't account for economic viability, transmission infrastructure needs, or seasonal variability. Some countries (small island states, landlocked Sahel) have limited diverse renewable portfolio. Data vintage: IRENA 2024 (capacity), Global Solar Atlas 2019-2021 (solar), Global Wind Atlas 2020 (wind), IRENA/WB various years (hydro potential).]

Data Sources: IRENA Renewable Capacity Statistics 2024 (installed capacity by technology), Global Solar Atlas (solar potential kWh/m²/day), Global Wind Atlas (wind potential), World Bank / IRENA (hydro potential TWh/year), World Bank (population for per capita calculations)

Access: IRENA Data | Global Solar Atlas | Global Wind Atlas

06

The Divergence

Some Escaping, Others Trapped

Question: Do electricity outcomes improve automatically as countries grow richer?

This animated scatter plot tracks countries over 13 years (2010-2023), plotting GDP per capita against electricity consumption per capita. Use the year slider to watch trajectories unfold. Some countries escape energy poverty while others remain trapped despite GDP growth. Economic growth does not guarantee rising electricity use—outcomes diverge persistently over time.

Key Insights

Kenya doubled consumption 150→350 kWh through integrated grid and off-grid programs. Rwanda tripled 50→145 kWh despite starting from genocide. Ethiopia grew through massive hydro investment. Meanwhile, Nigeria stagnated 145→160 kWh despite 4× Kenya's GDP per capita and oil revenues. Angola and DRC show similar patterns: GDP growth without electricity investment equals hollow development. The gap between Escapers and Trapped widened 2010-2023, not closed. Policy determines trajectories: grid + off-grid integration (Kenya), renewable investment (Ethiopia), and governance stability (Rwanda) enable escape. Resource wealth without infrastructure (Nigeria, Angola, DRC) creates traps.

Role in narrative: This chart shows the electricity gap is structural, not transitional. Time and economic growth alone do not fix the problem—outcomes persistently diverge based on policy choices.

Methodology & Data Challenges

Methodology

[PLACEHOLDER: Animated scatter uses year slider parameter (2010-2023) to filter data points by year. Trajectory lines created using "trail" mark type connecting each country's annual positions. Classification system: calculated electricity-GDP elasticity for each country (% change in consumption / % change in GDP) over 2010-2023 period. ESCAPERS: elasticity ≥0.5 (consumption growing at least 50% as fast as GDP—electricity investment keeping pace). STEADY: elasticity 0.25-0.5 (moderate progress). TRAPPED: elasticity <0.25 (severe electricity-GDP decoupling, falling further behind). Color coding: green=Escapers, yellow=Steady, red=Trapped. Point size encodes population. Tooltips show country name, year, exact GDP, consumption, and classification. GDP on log x-axis to accommodate range $500-$15,000 PPP.]

Data Challenges

[PLACEHOLDER: TEMPORAL COVERAGE - Not all countries have complete 2010-2023 series. Countries with >3 consecutive missing years excluded from trajectory analysis. South Sudan (independent 2011) only included from 2012 forward. LINEAR INTERPOLATION used for single-year gaps where data exists before/after. ELASTICITY CALCULATION sensitive to base year effects—countries starting from very low consumption may show high elasticity from small absolute gains. We used compound annual growth rate (CAGR) approach to smooth volatility. GDP in PPP terms reduces currency fluctuation effects but PPP conversion factors themselves updated periodically by World Bank, creating slight discontinuities. CONSUMPTION DATA doesn't distinguish GDP composition—oil-exporting economies may show high GDP with low residential consumption. Trajectory lines can obscure individual years—slider interaction addresses this. Some dramatic changes (e.g., civil conflicts, droughts affecting hydro) create outlier years that distort smooth trends. CLASSIFICATION THRESHOLDS (0.5, 0.25) chosen based on development economics literature suggesting 1:1 elasticity desirable for inclusive growth, but thresholds somewhat arbitrary. Countries near boundaries (0.24 vs 0.26) may be misclassified. Small population countries (e.g., Seychelles, Cabo Verde) show high volatility year-to-year, excluded or flagged as anomalies.]

Data Sources: World Bank World Development Indicators - GDP per capita PPP (NY.GDP.PCAP.PP.CD), Electric power consumption per capita (EG.USE.ELEC.KH.PC), Population (SP.POP.TOTL), Annual data 2010-2023

Access: World Bank DataBank

Data & Tools

Data Sources

This project relies entirely on publicly accessible, open data from international organizations and research institutions. Primary sources include: World Bank World Development Indicators (electricity access, consumption, GDP, population 2010-2023), International Energy Agency Statistics (tariffs, generation mix), IRENA Renewable Capacity Statistics 2024 (solar, wind, hydro installed capacity), World Bank Enterprise Surveys (generator ownership rates 2015-2023), Global Solar Atlas and Global Wind Atlas (renewable resource potential), and national electricity regulatory commission data where available.

All datasets were cleaned and merged in Python using ISO 3166 alpha-3 country codes for alignment. Temporal alignment addressed different data release schedules: WDI updates annually with 1-year lag, Enterprise Surveys run on 3-5 year cycles per country, IRENA releases capacity data annually. Missing data handled through: (a) forward-filling for slowly-changing variables like tariffs, (b) linear interpolation for consumption trends where single-year gaps exist, (c) exclusion where gaps exceeded 3 consecutive years. Final dataset covers 35-40 SSA countries with complete or near-complete data across all indicators.

Tools & Technologies

All visualizations built in Vega-Lite 5.x, a declarative grammar of interactive graphics, embedded in HTML via vegaEmbed. Chart 1: Parameter-driven choropleth with TopoJSON geographic data and toggle between access/consumption views. Charts 2-4: Layered encoding specifications with custom tooltips, reference lines, and annotations. Chart 5: Dual visualization with bubble scatter (left) and choropleth map (right) in side-by-side grid layout. Chart 6: Animated temporal scatter with year slider parameter and trajectory lines using trail marks.

Data processing workflow implemented in Python (pandas for data manipulation, statsmodels for regression analysis, requests for web scraping). Complete data processing pipeline documented in public Google Colab notebook. All chart specifications (Vega-Lite JSON), processed datasets (CSV), and TopoJSON geographic files hosted on GitHub repository for full reproducibility.

Analytical Approach

Key methodological decisions: (1) Consumption per capita preferred over total generation because it accounts for population size and reveals inequality invisible in aggregate figures. (2) Generator ownership as reliability proxy—Enterprise Survey data on backup generators provides standardized measure of grid failure across countries. (3) Logarithmic scaling for GDP in Charts 3 and 6 addresses right-skewed income distribution and emphasizes proportional relationships. (4) Regression analysis uses OLS with robust standard errors; Chart 3 includes 95% confidence intervals. (5) Temporal classification in Chart 6 based on electricity-GDP elasticity thresholds from development literature. (6) Diesel generation estimates calculated conservatively from generator ownership rates, average capacity assumptions, and outage duration—likely understates true parallel generation.

Conclusions

The Crisis is Not Inevitable

Africa's electricity challenge is not about lacking connections, income, or resources. It is about how electricity systems are priced, governed, and delivered. Six uncomfortable truths reveal the gap between perception and reality:

The Diagnostic Journey

  1. Access looks high → but hides deprivation (Chart 1: The Divide)
  2. Consumption is extremely low → modern energy is absent (Chart 2: Living on 3 Lightbulbs)
  3. Poverty alone cannot explain this → tariffs matter (Chart 3: The Injustice)
  4. System failure suppresses demand → reliability matters (Chart 4: The Hidden Economy)
  5. Resources are abundant → the constraint lies elsewhere (Chart 5: The Paradox)
  6. Time does not fix the problem → divergence persists (Chart 6: The Divergence)

Three Critical Findings

1
Access Percentages Mislead

Consumption inequality dwarfs connection inequality. South Africa's 4,000 kWh/capita versus Chad's 132 kWh/capita represents a 30× gap invisible in access rates. Policy should target minimum consumption thresholds (500 kWh/capita for basic productive use) not just connection counts. Otherwise we celebrate 100% connections while populations remain energy-poor.

2
Affordability and Reliability Crises Render Connections Unusable

Liberia charging $0.40/kWh for populations earning less than $4/day. Nigeria with 78% of firms self-generating through expensive diesel. Tariff reform (cross-subsidies, lifeline rates) and reliability investment (grid maintenance, storage) matter more than expanding unreliable, unaffordable systems. The poverty trap is real: low consumption → high fixed costs → unaffordable tariffs → lower consumption.

3
Investment Follows Institutional Capacity, Not Resource Potential

Nigeria's renewable underinvestment despite 5.5 kWh/m²/day solar irradiance reflects policy failure, not technical constraint. Chart 6's temporal dimension shows gaps widened 2010-2023, not converged—some countries escaped (Kenya, Rwanda, Ethiopia) through grid + off-grid strategies and policy reform, others stagnated (Nigeria, DRC, Angola) despite GDP growth and resource wealth. Electricity-GDP decoupling confirms energy as a binding growth constraint.

Policy Implications

International development frameworks (SDG7, African Development Bank's New Deal on Energy for Africa) should incorporate consumption, affordability, and reliability metrics alongside access targets. Proposed additions:

  • Consumption targets: Minimum 500 kWh/capita for productive use (refrigeration, small business equipment)
  • Affordability metrics: Tariff as % of median daily income, hours worked per 100 kWh
  • Reliability indicators: Average outage frequency/duration, % firms owning backup generators
  • Progress tracking: Annual electricity-GDP elasticity, divergence indices between countries

Need differentiated strategies: grid extension where viable (urban/peri-urban), off-grid solutions where cost-effective (rural, dispersed), but always with focus on sufficiency not just access. Kenya's integrated approach—grid expansion plus off-grid solar acceleration—demonstrates this is possible.

Together, these charts demonstrate that Africa's electricity crisis is not inevitable. Understanding that the problem is structural—about systems, not scarcity—is the first step toward designing solutions that actually work.
